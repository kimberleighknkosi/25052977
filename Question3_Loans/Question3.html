The brief

The Director wants to challenge three long-held beliefs and answer some practical questions about default risk. I work on settled loans only (outcome known: Fully Paid vs Charged Off / Default); 22.6% of them defaulted.

As odds ratios (>1 raises default odds), interest rate is the single clearest driver: each extra percentage point raises default odds by about 12%, and the 60-month term adds risk, while home ownership and long tenure pull it down.

Answering the Director

A sensible DTI cap. Default rate by debt-to-income band:

dti_band n default_rate
0-10 3389 0.172
10-20 7764 0.201
20-30 5777 0.258
30-40 2021 0.317
40+ 160 0.300

Risk rises steadily with DTI and is clearly worse above 30. A hard cap around DTI 30–35 is a defensible line: below it, default tolerance is reasonable; above it, risk accelerates. Different tolerance levels follow directly, e.g. cap at 25 to be conservative, 35 to be permissive.

Are the Institute’s heuristics realistic?

  • Owners / long-tenure default less: true but weak, with a real signal and a small effect.
  • States have a default culture: overstated; the differences are mostly borrower-mix.
  • Grade and rate predict default: strongly supported, and the most reliable of the three.

Who is most likely to default, and what reduces risk? The highest-risk profile is a low-grade, high-interest, 60-month loan to a renter; the lowest is a top-grade short-term loan to a long-tenure home owner. To reduce risk at assessment, lean on grade/interest rate and DTI (the variables that actually carry signal) rather than state or job tenure.

Limitation

The data has no borrower age, so the brief’s “younger borrowers” and “rates set by age/occupation” angles can’t be tested directly, and interest rate stands in as the risk signal. Figures use a 50,000-loan reproducible sample; directions are stable, exact rates shift slightly on the full data.
